What we're hearing across calls
General insights
Patterns on how to use AI most effectively for personal and professional efficiency, enablement, and competence
  • AI is the new "go do research on this" (n=18, all industries and functions). AI consistently replaces the first 1–3 hours of any research task — not the task itself. From VC deal screening (one firm went from 10 to 100 companies screened per hour) to first-pass market analysis and due diligence, output is treated as a starting point, not a final answer. "Go do a big research project and give me all of your sources — the sources themselves tell you a lot."
  • Connecting AI to your actual data is the real unlock (n=16). Without CRM, database, Slack, email, and calendar connected, enterprise AI is "a glorified chatbot." With connectors, use cases compound: meeting prep, account handoffs, deal screening, and financial automation all get meaningfully faster. The most frustrated users are almost always those stuck with unconnected tools. "The place I get the most value is connecting it to our data sources."
  • AI as always-on cognitive partner (n=14). The most effective individual users treat AI as an iterative thinking partner, not a one-shot output machine. They debate it, do a first pass, let it push back, and load it with personal context upfront. Key differentiator from basic chatbot use: the human-in-the-loop iterative loop. "I debate with it a lot. I'll do a first pass, let it iterate."
  • Automation of repeatable, structured workflows (n=13). Clearest ROI comes from high-frequency, structured, previously manual work: weekly deal newsletters, RFP population from a historical answer corpus, Excel diligence analyses, nightly expense logging. Method: decompose the process first ("put each step on a Post-it, go one at a time"). Rule of thumb: automate when you're "at the rinse and repeat stage."
  • Peer sharing is the primary learning channel (n=12). Informal channels — "build with AI" Slack channels, all-hands demos, hackathons — consistently spread AI capability faster than formal training programs. The most effective versions have psychological safety built in: channels that explicitly welcome failures alongside wins. "We have an active Slack channel: hey, I did something cool. Or, I totally failed here."
  • Forcing mechanisms drive real adoption (n=12). Optional encouragement consistently produces low adoption. High-uptake organizations all use explicit forcing: mandatory AI projects at weekly meetings, AI fluency in performance reviews, no-human-code experiments, ship-to-production hackathons. Mandating improvement without a mechanism just produces checkbox behavior. "You can't come to the meeting unless you have an AI project to show — and it can't be something simple."
Most Innovative Awards go to...
  • L1/L2/L3 Attribution Taxonomy: One cleantech startup requires every work product to declare its level of AI contribution: L1 = human-led with no AI; L2 = AI-assisted (human directed, AI executed); L3 = AI-generated (human reviewed, AI authored). Solves the "voice authenticity" problem, creates accountability, and gives the whole team a shared language for how much AI was actually involved. No one else in the dataset has formalized this.
  • Voice-Activated Ambient AI: One senior PM runs remote agents via voice while driving, walking, and during other daily activities — treating AI as an always-on operating mode rather than a discrete tool session.
  • Personal AI Lab at Home: One senior engineering leader built a rack-mount home server with AI managing smart shades (tracking sunrise, sunset, and sun angle), flight tracking via antenna, and home automation — treating personal life as an R&D environment for AI systems thinking.
Gotchas, learnings, failures
Things people wish they'd known earlier, mistakes that derailed AI deployments, and late realizations
  • Enterprise tool approval is a multi-month blocking event (n=15). Six months to approve a single tool is not an outlier — it's the median experience at companies above 1,000 employees. And it compounds: tool approval, connector approval, and MCP integration approval are three separate queues. Many teams end up with enterprise licenses that are effectively useless because nothing is connected. "It took us six months to get ChatGPT approved. We're hoping Claude takes less."
  • Access without training: the rollout that never arrives (n=14). The single most repeated deployment failure: secure a license, send a Slack message, point people at basic tutorials, consider the job done. Result: 5–10% who were already motivated adopt; 90% use it sporadically or not at all. Promising department-level tailored training that then gets cancelled is worse than saying nothing — it creates expectation, then withdraws it. "When they gave us access they were like, start exploring on your own. We're gonna have department-level sessions. But nothing's actually happened yet."
  • Tool churn anxiety (n=10). Early adopters are often the most anxious: they invest time mastering a tool, then it gets superseded. One ops leader went all-in on Cursor, then felt behind when Claude Code became the standard months later. Practical implication: learning tools is a less durable investment than learning principles — process decomposition, context management, and iterative loops transfer; specific tool fluency doesn't. "By the time I've built something, I'm like, oh, actually I could do this differently."
  • Shadow IT as a symptom of governance failure (n=8). Shadow AI is pervasive at organizations with strict IT governance — unapproved tools on personal accounts, off-the-books agents, recording all meetings in unapproved apps. Pattern: the more conservative the official AI policy, the more aggressive the workarounds. The individuals doing this are typically the most capable AI adopters in the org. "We built an AI agent that ingested all our historical application data — off the books, off the radar. We built it kind of illegally, but we did it." Addressing shadow IT requires creating legitimate fast lanes, not just stricter enforcement.
  • Generic AI courses fail because they don't map to actual workflows (n=7). The most consistent critique of existing training programs: too basic, not connected to participants' actual work, and delivered in formats that don't allow hands-on practice. Demo-only sessions, checkbox-completion gating, and generic content all produce the same outcome: low retention, no behavior change. "What they were teaching was basic but not relevant. It didn't help me understand how I was using AI in my real life."

Internal approaches — by frequency
  • AI champion / function sponsor model (n=10): Designate 1–5 power users per function who receive deeper training and disseminate to their team. Risk: champions burn out when it's added to a full existing workload without ring-fenced time.
  • Hackathon / structured sprint (incl. demo days & show-and-tell) (n=9): Time-boxed "pens down" sessions where teams build something together. Most effective when combined with hard calendar protection — protected time gets eroded otherwise.
  • Pilot group → broader rollout (n=8): Start with a small cohort of motivated users to generate internal case studies and enablement materials before rolling out org-wide.
  • Slack channel for peer sharing (n=8): Active channel for sharing wins, failures, and new tricks. Psychological safety (welcoming failures) is the key differentiator for channels that actually spread knowledge.
  • Train-the-trainer / champion → department (n=6): Champion receives deep training externally, then runs internal sessions for their function.
  • Mandatory AI projects / forcing mechanism (n=5): Weekly AI Huddles requiring a project to show, quarterly ship-to-production sprints, or other hard accountability mechanisms.
  • Bi-weekly / regular AI cadence meeting (n=4): Prevents AI adoption from being a one-time event. Recurring cadence is the common thread in orgs with sustained progress.

What's working vs. failing
  • Working: hands-on, workflow-specific practice. Formats where participants build something connected to their actual work during the session — not watch a demo, not hear a lecture. In-person setup alongside the learner, in context, with real tools and real data. The closer to real work, the higher the retention.
  • Working: champion + regular cadence. The most sophisticated internal upskilling structures in the dataset combine a designated full-time AI captain, bi-weekly AI virtuals with sharing, a formal attribution policy, and a CTO-built shared knowledge base. Multi-layered and recurring — not a one-time event.
  • Working: peer demo creates social proof. Seeing a peer do something useful makes the behavior feel achievable. A corporate training video doesn't achieve the same effect.
  • Failing: compliance-gated training = checkbox behavior. Training that functions as a prerequisite to unlock a license produces learners who do the minimum to get access, then use the tool however they were already inclined.
  • Failing: generic content without workflow context. "Basic but not relevant" is the consistent verdict on generic AI courses. The format that fails most is a demo-of-what's-possible that the learner can't immediately replicate in their own data and workflow environment.
  • Failing: access without integration = partial value. Providing tool access without solving the connector problem (data permissions, MCPs, approved integrations) produces chatbot-level usage and disengagement. The most preventable failure mode in the dataset.
Most Innovative Awards go to...
  • L1/L2/L3 Contribution Labeling: One cleantech startup mandates that all work products declare the level of AI contribution: L1 = human-led; L2 = AI-assisted; L3 = AI-generated. Creates accountability, honesty, and a shared vocabulary — and none of the external vendors in this dataset offer anything equivalent.
  • In-Person Portco Setup Days: One small PE firm sends its in-house AI champion to each portfolio company for a dedicated day — getting systems connected, building the first agent together, and leaving with a documented list of next automations. High-touch, high-retention, no curriculum required.
  • Agent Productionization Pipeline: One tech company built a dedicated team that evaluates individual contributors' agents, productionizes the best ones, and distributes them org-wide via a menu. Scales the work of the most advanced builders without requiring everyone to be an expert.
