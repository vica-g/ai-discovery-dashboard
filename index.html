Emerging Patterns (n=28)

  • "Enterprise tool licensed ≠ adoption" is the core gap (n=5+): Section AI at Asurion (checkbox to get ChatGPT, then forgotten), Activision (OpenAI deployed but no workflow change), Datavant (OpenAI Enterprise org-wide but Claude blocked except in terminal), Gusto (non-R&D teams still basic despite massive investment), AppFolio (150-person Claude pilot with daily monitoring but training = "go play"). Having the tool approved is the beginning, not the end.
  • Chatbot-level users = sweet spot for L1 (n=9+): Christian, Seth, Aidan, Mansoor, Jack, Omar, Michael, Pek Nan, Johanna, Cassie, Zeinab, Eliane, and Jonah are all at chatbot level. The more advanced the person (Suchit = agents, Laura = automating), the less likely they are to buy — though Andrew (automating) and Nick (agents) are notable exceptions where portco/team leverage overrides personal advancement.
  • Companies with large non-engineering workforces = warmest prospects (n=4): Flynn (400-person back office), Rula (300-450 non-eng), Asurion (non-technical functions), Gusto (CS, support, sales, legal). The common thread isn't industry — it's having hundreds of non-technical employees who need upskilling but can't self-serve.
  • "Even advanced orgs have an underserved middle" (n=4): Gusto, DoorDash, Ammobia, and Asurion all have the same pattern: R&D/product/eng races ahead while non-technical teams are left behind. Even with a dedicated head of AI enablement, AI stipends, and performance axes, the benefits are uneven. Our entry point at advanced orgs is the specific teams the internal enablement org hasn't reached.
  • "Can't build training in-house" = buying trigger (n=5+): Flynn has nobody internally who can teach AI. Rula has 1 L&D person focused on onboarding. Stepful has no AI champion and no roadmap. Cassie's Rainforest Builder has no one in charge of AI integration. AppFolio's only training: a Slack channel + "do the Anthropic 101." Innova Schools has no internal AI operations training despite building AI products. When there's no internal trainer, we're the obvious solution.
  • InfoSec pre-approval = onboarding friction (n=3): Johanna (Howgood), Christian (Flynn), and Aidan (Rula) flagged compliance/InfoSec timelines as a risk. Johanna's insight: submit a scoped connector list to InfoSec before day 1. Worth building a formal pre-cohort checklist to reduce drop-off.
  • Fund/portfolio distribution = scale channel (n=3): Nick (Growth Street, 10 portcos), Michael (Riverspan, 3 portcos), and now Abigail (Breakthrough Energy, ~80 portcos) all see the same model: one fund relationship → training seats across many portfolio companies. BEV's scale is an order of magnitude larger (160-240 potential seats vs. 50-100). The pattern extends beyond PE — philanthropic/venture funds with portfolio structures have the same leverage. Nick has Skylar (internal AI champion); Michael has no champion; Abigail IS the champion for an entire climate tech portfolio.
  • HIPAA / data compliance = emerging moat (n=3): Rula (Aidan), Datavant (Andrew), and Cordis (David) are in regulated healthcare facing IT-enforced HIPAA constraints. If we can demonstrate data-sanitized workflows and healthcare-safe prompting, that's a real differentiator generic vendors won't have.
  • Public company oversight = slow but large (n=2): AppFolio (Zeinab) and DoorDash (Laura) are both public. DoorDash moved in 6 days; AppFolio has daily usage reports and legal monitoring. Different speeds, same eventual destination. The AppFolio pattern — formal pilot → monitoring → enterprise decision — is likely more common among public companies. If the 1,500-person rollout lands, the deal size dwarfs anything else in pipeline.

Emerging ICP

  • Org: 250–2,000 employees. Large non-engineering workforce (ops, finance, legal, support, marketing). No dedicated internal AI trainer. Leadership has signaled AI matters but hasn't operationalized it. Industry-agnostic so far (Healthcare, Financial Services, F&B all green); what matters is workforce composition, not sector.
  • Persona: Head of Ops or equivalent (VP/Director-level). Personally at chatbot stage — using AI but knows they should be doing more. Either already the decision-maker for upskilling (Aidan) or highly motivated to become the internal champion (Seth, Mansoor). Reports to COO or CFO. Has or can unlock an L&D / AI transformation budget.
  • Red flags: Tech-forward CEO/leadership (they'll self-serve). Company <50 people (too small, informal knowledge sharing suffices). Fully generic Microsoft/Copilot-only environment with no external vendor appetite.

Methodology Builds

  • Healthcare validating as vertical (n=3): Rula, Datavant, and Cordis are all warm, regulated healthcare companies where HIPAA constrains tools but doesn't kill interest. None were cold leads. Worth pursuing deliberately: regulated healthcare orgs with 500-2K employees and non-engineering workforces.
  • Fund/portfolio distribution pattern (n=3): Nick (Growth Street, PE, 10 portcos), Michael (Riverspan, PE, 3 portcos), and Abigail (Breakthrough Energy, philanthropy/VC, ~80 portcos). The model extends beyond PE — any fund with portfolio companies has the same distribution leverage. BEV's 80-company portfolio is the largest potential play. Worth deliberately targeting more fund managers (PE, VC, impact, accelerator) as a distinct sales motion.
  • Channel partner conversations emerging (n=2): Jim (US enterprise through cybersecurity partners) and Metin (Turkish market through former CIO of Turkish Airlines). First international channel partner. Different from direct B2B — requires rev share framework and one-pager before pipeline develops.
  • Industry coverage wide but thin: 11 industries represented across 27 calls. Financial Services at n=4, Tech at n=9 (largest cluster), Education at n=2 (Stepful + Innova). Most sectors at n=1-2. Too early to rule verticals in or out — need 2-3 data points each before drawing ICP conclusions by industry.
  • Mid-market (250-999) still undersampled: Only Rula at n=1 in the 250-999 range — and it's our strongest prospect. Need more calls in 50-999 range to pressure-test whether mid-market is truly the sweet spot.
  • "Paid for AI courses that didn't stick" = high-intent signal (n=2): Eliane (Innova) paid out of pocket for an AI course — "basic but not relevant, never stuck." Mackenzie (Clarify) paid $600 for Recalc AI. Both are frustrated with generic training. When someone has already spent money and been disappointed, the bar is clearer: they want hands-on, applied, in their own workflows. That's exactly our pitch.
  • International / LatAm prospects emerging (n=1): Eliane is the first LatAm-based prospect. AI adoption is "much slower outside the US" per her observation. Innova is part of Intercorp (major Peruvian conglomerate). Recorded sessions + async TA model is a natural fit for timezone differences. Worth watching whether $199 F&F pricing hits differently in non-US markets.
  • Manager-level buying triggers differ (n=2): Laura (DoorDash) and Sarah J. (Gusto) are both Managers at advanced orgs. Both acted as connectors/referrers rather than direct buyers — passing to AI leads or L&D teams. Director+ prospects are more likely to be the decision-maker themselves.
  • Hackathons as forcing functions (n=1): Sarah R. (Outset) is setting up a GTM hackathon next Thursday for 20-25 people. If it goes well, it validates AI adoption internally and creates appetite for structured training. If messy, the need for external structure becomes obvious. Either outcome benefits us. Worth asking other prospects whether hackathons are on their calendar — a natural "before/after" comparison point for our product.
