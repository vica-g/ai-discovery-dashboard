Emerging Patterns (n=6)

  • Companies with large non-engineering workforces = warmest prospects: Flynn (back office), Asurion (non-technical functions), and Rula (ops, finance, legal, marketing, support) are all interested and sharing with teams. The common thread isn't industry — it's having hundreds of non-technical employees who need upskilling but can't self-serve.
  • Tech-forward orgs/leaders = red flag: Suchit (builds agents, will self-serve) and Clarify (AI-native culture, too small). They'll figure it out themselves.
  • Chatbot-level users = sweet spot: Christian, Seth, and Aidan are all chatbot-only users who want to go further but need structure. The more advanced the person (Suchit = agents, Mackenzie = automating), the less likely they are to buy.
  • "Can't build training in-house" = clear buying trigger: Rula has 1 L&D person focused on onboarding. Flynn has nobody internally who can teach AI. Both are active opportunities. When there's no internal trainer, we're the obvious solution.

Methodology Builds

  • Mid-market gap partially filled (n=1): Rula at 600 employees is the first data point in the 250-999 range — and our strongest prospect. Need more calls here before drawing conclusions, but early signal is very strong.
  • Prioritize companies with large non-engineering workforces: 3 green/dark-green data points (Flynn, Asurion, Rula) share this trait across different industries (Food & Bev, Financial Services, Healthcare). The ICP signal is the workforce composition, not the industry label. Rula is healthtech but still fits — most of their 600 employees are non-technical.
  • Target chatbot-level users, not power users: All 3 training prospects (Christian, Seth, Aidan) are chatbot-only users. Both people further along (Suchit = agents, Mackenzie = automating workflows) opted out. Screen for "using AI but only as a chatbot" as the clearest ICP signal.
